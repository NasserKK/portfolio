Accessibility Improvement — Nubank App

Independent UX accessibility study applying Human-Centered Design principles to improve the Nubank mobile app experience for users with visual impairments, focusing on the Home screen.

HERO IMAGE — Nubank App (from PDF)

Overview

This project aimed to apply Human-Centered Design to identify and propose accessibility improvements in the Nubank mobile application. The focus was on users with visual impairments and on the app’s Home screen, the main entry point for core financial actions.

The study was conducted independently and structured as an accessibility audit, usability testing, and UX redesign proposal.

Accessibility Context

Accessibility goes beyond visual contrast or font size. It is about removing barriers that prevent people from interacting fully with digital products. In financial apps, accessibility is especially critical, as it directly impacts autonomy, trust, and inclusion.

This case focused on users with visual needs, grouped into:

  • Color blindness (color vision deficiency)
  • Blind users relying on screen readers
  • Low vision users who require assistive technologies

Research & Testing

To understand the real experience of users with visual impairments, I conducted moderated accessibility testing with four volunteers.

During the sessions:

  • Participants were blindfolded
  • Only the screen reader was allowed
  • Real tasks were performed on the Nubank app

Tasks Tested

  • Check credit card limit
  • Hide account balance
  • Access the investments area

While the first two tasks were completed successfully, all participants failed to complete the third task.

USABILITY TESTING PHOTOS / TASK SCREENS

Key Insight & Solution

The issue was not related to visual contrast or typography — Nubank already meets WCAG Level AAA contrast standards.

The problem was semantic and structural: the “Shortcuts” area did not communicate, via screen reader, that it was a horizontally scrollable list with additional options.

Design Principle

Improve accessibility without compromising the visual interface. Small semantic changes can unlock major usability gains.

  • Explicit screen reader guidance (“Swipe right to explore”)
  • Item position feedback (“1 of 9”, “2 of 9”)
  • Clear identification of interactive elements

Impact

Although this was an independent study, based on usability testing results and accessibility best practices, the proposed solution would plausibly lead to:

100%

task completion for screen reader users in the shortcuts area

0

visual UI changes required

High

accessibility compliance improvement

Learnings

  • Accessibility issues are often semantic, not visual.
  • Screen reader experience must be intentionally designed.
  • Small changes can unlock major usability improvements.
  • Inclusive design improves the product for everyone.
  • Accessibility should be part of the design process, not a final checklist.
