UX/UI Case — RCHLO Quality System

Digital transformation of quality control processes (paper → web) for factory and workshop operations, designed for tablet-first usage.

Overview

The RCHLO Quality System was an internal platform created to replace manual, paper-based quality control routines. It was used by the Quality team, controllers, and professionals in factories and workshops. The product was built as a web application with a tablet-first experience, supporting operational usage in production environments.

Problem Context

Before RCHLO, quality inspections and checklists were commonly recorded on paper. This generated frequent operational issues:

  • Rework caused by incomplete or incorrect forms.
  • Audit difficulty due to the lack of centralized and traceable data.
  • Inconsistent standards across different factories and workshops.
  • Filling errors without validation rules or mandatory fields.
  • Illegible handwriting compromising data accuracy and decision-making.

My Role

I worked as the sole UX/UI Designer on the project, leading design decisions in close partnership with the client and developers. Research with real users was continuous throughout the process, following frameworks such as Design Thinking and the Double Diamond.

1. Research & Discovery

Frequent interviews with controllers and operational users to understand constraints, errors, and real workflows.

2. Flow Mapping (co-creation)

Workflows were built from factory flowcharts provided by managers and refined through user conversations.

3. Prototyping in Figma

Rapid prototyping to explore multiple solution paths, refine interactions, and reduce ambiguity for development.

4. Testing & Iteration

Validation with real users who depended on the feature daily, ensuring the solution fit the operational reality.

Key Flow — Checklist Correction Request

One of the most sensitive challenges was enabling the correction of checklist fields without compromising audit integrity. In practice, many checklists were rejected due to simple filling mistakes (e.g., measurements or defect quantities). Allowing direct edits would open space for bad-faith behavior, where users could change values just to force approval.

The Challenge

  • Enable legitimate corrections to avoid unnecessary re-inspections.
  • Prevent fraud and data manipulation.
  • Maintain traceability and audit readiness.

Solution

  • The auditor submits a correction request instead of editing directly.
  • The request is sent to the area manager for review and approval.
  • Changed fields are highlighted in yellow and marked with a “!”.
  • Clicking the marker shows previous value vs proposed value.
  • A mandatory justification is required in all cases.
  • For defect-related changes, photo attachments are mandatory.

Impact

The project did not start with formal analytics instrumentation. However, based on operational feedback and observed workflow improvements, we estimated conservative, realistic outcomes after implementation:

20–30%

reduction in checklist-related rework

15–25%

fewer re-inspections caused by filling errors

High

increase in traceability and audit readiness

Learnings

  • Designing for risk and fraud prevention in enterprise workflows.
  • Using UX as a tool for governance and traceability, not only usability.
  • Co-creating with specialist users and translating real operational processes into digital flows.
  • Balancing flexibility and control in sensitive features.
  • Leading end-to-end design decisions as the sole designer and negotiating with multiple stakeholders.
  • Ensuring product continuity even when the client-side PM changed mid-sprint.
